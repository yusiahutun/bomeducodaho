How To Get Gorgeous Curls

October 2024 · 4 minute read

Curly hair is having a moment. "It is very on-trend to wear your hair au naturel—it's contemporary and chic," says Eugene Souleiman, global creative director for Wella Professionals, who crafted the texture-driven styles at Chloé and Stella McCartney. Here, experts weigh in on what you need to know about cutting, styling, and caring for your coils.

THE BEST CUTS

If your hairstylist said, "Maybe we should try bangs?" your instinct as a curly-haired woman might be to bolt from the chair and never look back. But curly hair and curly bangs are the hot new duo. Think edgy Argentine model Mica Arganaraz's rock 'n' roll shag. "It's the coolest look, and you can't get it with straight hair," says stylist Tommy Buckett of New York's Serge Normant at John Frieda salon. "This style is not about the perfect curl. Before, curls were so contrived and defined, and now they're just loose and super sexy."

But bangs or no bangs, getting the right cut is critical. A tousled, chin-length bob or lob that hits at the collarbone and is cut slightly shorter in the back feels totally modern, Souleiman says. "You want the curls to feel constant but with layers throughout to add dimension." A good rule of thumb? The tighter the curls, the longer the layers should be.

And a word of warning: Do not let anyone come anywhere near your spirals with a razor or thinning shears, says New York stylist Nunzio Saviano. "Cutting with a razor makes it look like you have split ends because it shreds the ends of the hair."

SHAMPOO SECRETS

Because curly hair tends to be drier than straight hair, many women take the no-poo approach and avoid shampoo, especially those with sulfates, chemical foaming agents that can fry your tresses. But experts agree: Shampooing, at least weekly, is essential for scalp health and hair growth. Stylist Kim Kimble, who maintains Beyoncé's leonine curls, recommends co-washing, or using a conditioner or conditioner/mild-suds hybrid, in between shampoos. "To seal in hydration, you can condition before you shampoo, which also helps detangle the hair." Try K2 Beauty Untangle Conditioner ($15) or Kérastase Paris Discipline Cleansing Conditioner Curl Idèal ($48), both of which are sulfate-free.

You can further prevent dryness by rotating your shampoos. Rodney Cutler, a co-owner of the Licari Cutler salon in New York, suggests using a sulfate-free one with minimal suds to help retain moisture, like Redken Curvaceous Low-Foam Moisturizing Cleanser ($18.50). "Then maybe once a week, when you want to scrub, use a higher-foam option to remove product buildup, which weighs down the hair," he says. OriginalMineral O&M Original Detox Shampoo ($30.95) does the job without stripping your hair's natural oils.

STYLING TRICKS

One of the hardest things about caring for curly hair is dealing with the frizziness that often accompanies it. Avoid towel-drying, which exacerbates frizz. Instead, both Saviano and Souleiman recommend gently blotting wet hair with a paper towel to soak up excess moisture. Impractical though it may be, this trick does work. "Paper towels absorb more water out of your hair than a regular towel without roughing it up," explains Saviano.

Next, while your hair is still damp, work in your styling products of choice, using your fingers to comb them through your lengths. Mousses and gels are supereffective at smoothing thick, unruly curls but can make your hair feel crunchy. Neutralize it by opting for alcohol-free versions (try L'Oréal Paris's EverStyle range) and using a smaller amount, then soften crispy areas with a little hair oil, says Souleiman. He likes Wella Professionals Luxe Oil Light Oil Keratin Protection Shine Spray ($28).

For looser spirals or finer hair texture, both Kimble and Buckett love cream formulas, which still provide hold but don't add bulk. "Don't be afraid of using more product than what it says on the back of the bottle," Buckett says. Garnier Fructis Curl Stretch Loosening Pudding ($4.29) and R+Co Twister Curl Primer ($25) are lightweight and smell divine.

Twist sections near the root and the mid-lengths around your fingers for definition, then let hair air-dry—and leave it alone. "The more you touch it, the more frizz you'll end up having," Saviano warns. After hair is dry, if necessary, add spring to limp locks with a curling iron that works for your curl type.

If your curls are damaged (excessive heat styling and chemical processing are the usual causes), you may notice straw-like, broken, or uneven strands that never seem to grow and excess shedding. You have two options: "You can start by babying your hair and using a product to mend the hair shaft," says Kimble. (Living Proof Fresh Cut Split End Mender, $24, works quickly to reinforce weak areas and can be used daily.) "Or you're just going to have to cut it and give it a chance to grow back." Silver lining: Now you have a reason to give those new bangs a try.
